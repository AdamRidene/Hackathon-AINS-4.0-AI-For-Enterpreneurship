# Scoring methodology

Firasa computes five composite scores on a 0–100 scale using a Gated Weighted Linear Combination (GWLC). Each score is a weighted sum of normalised criteria — the *base score* — followed by an optional non-linear *gate* that enforces a hard structural constraint. Every score is anchored to an established framework, and every score returns a per-criterion contribution trace so that the result is fully explainable. The weights live in one place, `app/scoring/weights.py`, so re-calibration is a configuration change rather than a code change.

## The five scores

**Market (S_M)** is anchored to Lean Startup validated learning. The base score combines three sub-dimensions: a log-normalised total addressable market at 0.40, `min(ln T / ln 10⁷, 1) · 100`; a competition term at 0.30, `max(0, 100 − 5·competitors)`; and revenue-model viability at 0.30, a binary `100 if a structured revenue model is declared else 0`. The third sub-dimension was added so that the score satisfies the rubric requirement of at least three visible sub-criteria and so that a large but un-monetisable market cannot inflate the score on TAM alone. Its gate is a hard cap: if there is no customer-validation token, the score is capped at 30 no matter how large the market.

**Commercial (S_C)** is anchored to the Strategyzer Value Proposition Canvas. It weights value-proposition coherence (the P_coh index supplied by the LLM-as-a-Judge, with a deterministic rubric fallback) at 0.30, MVP readiness at 0.40, and pricing coherence at 0.30.

**Innovation (S_I)** is anchored to the OECD Oslo Manual 2018. It weights geographic novelty at 0.40, technology-stack depth at 0.30, and IP status at 0.30, using ordinal maps for the categorical inputs (for example geographic novelty maps Reproduction → 10, Local-Opt → 40, Tunisian First-Mover → 80, Global → 100).

**Scalability (S_S)** is anchored to venture-capital marginal-cost decoupling. It weights operating-cost decoupling at 0.40, cross-border geographic reach at 0.30, and frictionless deployment at 0.30. Operating-cost decoupling is `clamp(100 − C_month / 20 000 · 100)`, so a near-zero monthly overhead scores ~100 and an overhead at or above 20 000 TND/month scores 0. Frictionless deployment is `(10 − human_dependency) / 10 · 100`, the inverse of how much incremental human effort growth demands. Its gate is a 0.5 multiplier applied when human dependency exceeds 7, encoding that a labour-bound operation does not scale regardless of its cost structure. The deliberate departure from the concept note's Equation 8 is explained below.

**Green (S_G)** is anchored to the UN SDG and World Bank ESG frameworks. It weights environmental footprint at 0.30, circularity at 0.40, and SDG coverage at 0.30.

## Weight justification and calibration

The weights are initialised from the expert judgement encoded in the Firasa concept (Section 6). They are intentionally centralised so the calibration methodology can re-fit them against the labelled test set using a constrained linear regression that preserves the unit-sum constraint (the weights within each score sum to 1). Keeping the weights as data, not logic, makes the weighting auditable and the re-fit reproducible. The current values reflect the relative importance the domain framework places on each criterion — for example, MVP readiness carries the most weight in the commercial score because a working product is the strongest signal of commercial progress, and circularity leads the green score because it is the most actionable lever in the Tunisian agri-industrial context.

## The gate model

A gate is a non-linear override applied *after* the base score, never folded into it, so that the base computation and the override remain separately explainable. Two gates are active. The market validation cap fixes the score at 30 when the validation token is absent or false. The scalability penalty multiplies the base by 0.5 when human dependency is above the threshold of 7. Each `ScoreResult` exposes `gate_triggered` and `gate_reason`, so the interface can show both the base score and the gated final score and explain the difference. This is the structural defence against a venture masking a fundamental weakness with strength elsewhere.

## Deliberate deviations from the concept note's worked example

The concept note's worked example (Section 6.6) describes an agri-tech venture with T = 4 000 000 TND, six competitors, validation present, and reports the five scores as (79.8, 71.1, 68.0, 29.2, 69.8). Three of the five — Commercial 71.1, Innovation 68.0, and Green 69.8 — reproduce *exactly* from the stated formulas, and the test suite asserts each unchanged. Market and Scalability deviate deliberately, for two distinct reasons.

**Scalability: a rewritten decoupling direction (Equation 8).** As written, the note's Equation 8 computes the decoupling term as `C_up / (C_up + C_month) · 100` — the capital-expenditure share of total cost. That rewards a *high* up-front capital cost and *punishes* a low recurring cost, which is backwards for the principle the score is anchored to. Marginal-cost decoupling is precisely the ability to grow revenue without growing recurring cost, so an asset-light software venture with near-zero monthly overhead is the *most* scalable, yet Equation 8 scores it near zero. We rewrote the term to reward low operating cost directly (`clamp(100 − C_month / 20 000 · 100)`) and added a third sub-dimension, frictionless deployment, as the inverse of human dependency. For the worked example this changes the base from the note's reading to **26.0**, gated by the human-dependency multiplier (D_man = 8 > 7) to a final **13.0** — close to the note's gated 29.2 in spirit (a low, penalised score) but computed on a defensible formula. The deviation is a one-line baseline change in `app/scoring/weights.py` (`OPEX_BASELINE`) if a different calibration is preferred, and `tests/test_scoring.py::test_scalability_three_subdims_and_gate` pins exactly what is computed.
